1.1 Scope the Project
Purpose The project is scoped to ensure that critical measures of success are identified, client needs and expectations are understood, and that sufficient funding is available. Account Management authorization to begin work is a prerequisite for scoping the project. 

The output of a successful project scoping is: 

  • Client needs and expectations are understood and documented 
  • Scope, or project description, is understood and documented 
  • Funding is made available to begin/continue 
Entrance Criteria Authorization to begin scoping
Charge to Codes Assigned 
Steps
  1. Assign, and thus appoint, a Project Management Director, or Designee (Account Management, Development Director). 
  2. Form a small team to begin the scoping phase (Account Manager). 
  3. Solicit and document client needs and expectations, or Scope of Work (Account Manager). 
  4. Identify critical measures of success and present the material as a business opportunity, with a business solution. Identify standard or high-level milestones. (Account Manager). 
  5. Use the Estimation Spreadsheets, or equivalent, as a beginning to the estimation process (Project Manager). 
  6. Identify and document risks and mitigation plans (Project Manager). 
  7. Document assumptions, including constraints, impacts on the current environment, and barriers (Project Manager). 
  8. Document Subcontract or Supplier Management Plan (if applicable) (Project Manager). 
  9. Develop a high-level Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) using an appropriate tracking tool (Project Manager). 
  10. Document items for the Pre-Commitment Project Plan (Account Manager). 
  11. Review the Pre-Commitment Project Plan with Project Team members for a baselined decision. After baselining, the Pre-Commitment Project Plan is a controlled document and is placed under change control (Account Manager). 
  12. Retain the appropriate artifacts in the project library for easy retrieval (Project Manager). 
Exit Criteria Pre-Commitment Project Plan (includes Statement of Work [SOW], risks, estimation, etc.)

1.3 Develop Thin Requirements
Purpose Thin Requirements are developed from client needs and business objectives. They cover not only the desired functionality for the system, but also address non-functional items (overall system quality, characteristics, or conditions) and non-technical items (management constraints or expectations). They are developed with client participation and are accepted by the client. 

Thin Requirements use the Requirements template to establish a common understanding between the client and the project team, to confirm agreement on the expectations, and as input to solidify the estimates. 

The purpose of requirements development and management is to establish a common understanding, between the client and the project team, of the requirements that will be addressed by the project. 

It is important to maintain control over the Requirements since they form the basis for the project's activities. 
Entrance Criteria Key Resources assigned, e.g., Analyst, Developers, etc.

 
Steps
  1. Uniquely identify each client requirement (Analyst/Engineer). 

    2. Document: 
    • functional requirements 
    • non-functional requirements 
    • technical requirements 
    • non-technical requirements 
    • client acceptance criteria 
  2. Review Thin Requirements with affected project team members (Project Manager). 
  3. Review Thin Requirements with client constituency (Project Manager). 
  4. Conduct internal baseline review for the Thin Requirements (Project Manager).

    5. (See Baseline Process, Change Control Process, Document Control, and
    Reviews in the Appendix section for details.) After commitment is entered the Thin Requirements document becomes a controlled document and is placed under change control. 
    This artifact uniquely identifies client requirements (needs). Scope changes can now be managed from this artifact. 
  5. Retain the appropriate artifacts in the Project Library for easy retrieval (Project Manager).

1.4 Pre-Commitment Architecture Specification
Purpose The Pre-Commitment Architecture Specification defines/documents the high-level, logical view of the system or specific architectural changes to an existing system or systems in order to achieve business objectives. The entire Pre-Commitment Architecture Specification is required for new projects. Extensively changed systems or new feature/Change Request (CR) work that impact architecture requires producing a document that addresses the approach taken in solving the incremental business requirements. 

The Pre-Commitment Architecture Specification establishes and communicates the approach proposed for implementing new system capabilities. The Pre-Commitment Architecture Specification is a point in time deliverable to satisfy the Company IT Software Development Commitment and Delivery Management (C&DM) process. The Pre-Commitment Architecture Specification will be the basis for producing or updating later Architecture Specification documents. 

The Pre-Commitment Architecture Specification will address: 

  • What systems are impacted 
  • Access mechanisms to existing systems 
  • Reuse of existing components or artifacts 
  • Identification and design approach for major subsystem components 
  • Distribution of processing elements 
  • Identification of significant data elements 
Entrance Criteria Baselined Thin Requirements 
Steps
  1. Capture the logical view of the Pre-Commitment Architecture Specification or changes to the architecture. (Development) Consider: 
    • An overview of the system, identifying the mission of the system and design objectives set forth in order to fulfill this mission 
    • Assumptions or constraints 
    • Strategies for meeting human factors objectives 
    • High level performance requirements 
    • Hardware needs 
    • Software needs 
    • Subsystems and functions/process control 
    • Major internal and external interface relationships 
    • Common routines 
    • Database considerations 
    • Outstanding issues and/or future considerations 
    • Installation, growth, and retrofit consideration 
  2. Conduct external review of the Pre-Commitment Architecture Specification through the IT-SD architecture review process. 
  3. Conduct internal review (Development). See Baseline Process, Change Control Process, Document Control, and Reviews in the Appendix section for details. 
  4. Retain appropriate artifacts in the project library for easy retrieval (Project Manager).

1.5 Create the Project Plan
Purpose The Project Plan is created to transition the Pre-Commitment Project Plan information to the Baselined Project Plan to represent how the project will be managed. A Project Plan must be baselined to begin any work effort. 
Entrance Criteria Pre-Commitment Project Plan
Baselined Thin Requirements 
Steps
  1. Using the Pre-Commitment Project Plan as basis, expand to include: (Project Manager)
    • additional detail to help manage the project 
    • communications strategies and plans for the project team, client(s), and other key stakeholders 
    • roles and responsibilities 
    • updated risks and mitigation plans for client satisfaction, business objectives, cost, schedule, technical resources, suppliers, etc. 
    • staffing plan 
    • subcontractor management decisions 
    • frequency of project status meetings and other informal status meetings 
    • project metrics, such as, size (estimated), cost, effort (staff and schedule/time) 
    • critical computer resources, and other resources 
    • more detailed WBS for upcoming phases 
    • defined Measures of Success (MOS) 
  2. Conduct internal baseline review for the Project Plan (Project Manager).

    3. See Baseline Process, Change Control Process, Document Control, and
    Reviews in the Appendix section for details. 
    The Project Plan is now a controlled document under change control. 
  3. Retain the appropriate artifacts in the project library for easy retrieval (Project Manager). 
  4. Finalize project staffing (Project Manager). 
  5. Initiate Project Status Meetings to monitor the progress of the project (Project Manager).

1.7 Conduct Project Kick-Off Meeting
Purpose The Project Kick-Off Meeting is conducted to assemble the project team and disseminate the project scope, risks, and success criteria. 

A Project Plan must be baselined to begin any work effort. 
Entrance Criteria Baselined Project Plan 
Steps
  1. Conduct Project Kick-Off Meeting to review the Project Plan with the Project Team (Project Manager).
  2. Socialize the Executive Summary, Critical Success Factors, Risks and Mitigation Plans, Estimates, Milestone(s), and WBS (Project Manager). 
    • Document Matrix or external resource commitments (if applicable). 
  3. Solicit feedback from the team (Project Manager). 
  4. Expand, update, or modify the Project Plan, and other affected documents as necessary with additional information from the Project Kick-Off Meeting (Project Manager). 
  5. Conduct internal baseline review for the Project Plan if required due to changes from the kick-off meeting (Project Manager).

    6. See Baseline Process, Change Control Process, Document Control, and
    Reviews in the Appendix section for details (if necessary). 
    The Project Plan is now a controlled document under change control. 
  6. Retain the appropriate artifacts in the Project Library for easy retrieval (Project Manager). 
  7. Socialize the final Project Staffing. 
  8. Provide sufficient data to other affected Project Team members (Project Manager). 
  9. Use Project Status Meetings to monitor the progress of the project (Project Manager).
